5 Google Business Profile Mistakes Killing Your Local Visibility

Chris Gallagher March 22, 2026 6 min read

Here's the thing about Google Business Profile mistakes: most business owners don't even know they're making them. You claimed your profile, filled out the basics, maybe uploaded a photo or two, and moved on. Totally understandable — you have a business to run.

But while you're out doing great work for your customers, your GBP listing might be quietly pushing you down in local search results. And the business that shows up instead of you? They might not be better at what they do. They just have a better-optimized profile.

After working with local service businesses on their Google presence, I keep seeing the same five mistakes over and over. The good news is every single one of them is fixable — usually in less than an afternoon.

Mistake #1: Using Your Business Name Wrong

What it looks like

This goes both ways. Some businesses stuff their name field with keywords — something like "Dave's Plumbing | Best Plumber in Cleveland | 24/7 Emergency Plumbing Services." Others go too generic and just put "Dave's" without any indication of what they actually do.

Why it hurts

Google takes your business name field seriously. If you stuff it with keywords, you're violating Google's guidelines and risking a suspension — they've been cracking down hard on this. If your name is too vague, you're missing the chance to help Google understand what you do (if your legal business name naturally includes your service, that's fine to use).

How to fix it

Use your real, legal business name. That's it. If your business is legally called "Dave's Plumbing Services," use exactly that. Don't add city names, extra keywords, or marketing taglines. And don't shorten it to something unrecognizable either. Google can and does verify business names, and getting flagged or suspended is a nightmare you don't want to deal with.

Mistake #2: Choosing the Wrong Primary Category

What it looks like

A pressure washing company that has their primary category set to "Cleaning Service." A roofer listed as "Contractor." An HVAC company categorized as "Home Improvement Store." These are real examples I've seen.

Why it hurts

Your primary category is one of the single biggest factors in whether you show up in local search results. When someone searches "pressure washing near me," Google looks at primary categories first to decide who to show. If yours says "Cleaning Service" instead of "Pressure Washing Service," you're fighting an uphill battle before anything else even matters.

How to fix it

Go to your GBP dashboard and check your primary category right now. It should be the most specific, accurate description of your core service. Google has hundreds of categories — don't settle for a broad one when a specific one exists. Then add relevant secondary categories for your other services. A pressure washer might add "Gutter Cleaning Service" and "Window Cleaning Service" as secondary categories to capture those searches too.

Mistake #3: Ignoring Your Reviews

What it looks like

You've got 15 reviews, most of them from two years ago. Some have no owner response at all. Maybe a negative review is sitting there unanswered from six months back. Or maybe you have great customers but you've never actually asked them to leave a review.

Why it hurts

Reviews are the biggest trust signal for both Google and your potential customers. Google uses review quantity, quality, and recency as direct ranking factors for the Local Pack. A business with 50 recent reviews will almost always outrank one with 12 old reviews — even if the 12-review business does better work. And that unanswered negative review? It tells every potential customer that you either don't care or you're not paying attention.

How to fix it

Build a simple review system you can actually stick with. After every completed job, send your customer a direct link to your Google review page (you can create a short link right from your GBP dashboard). Respond to every single review — positive and negative. For positive reviews, a genuine thank-you goes a long way. For negative ones, respond professionally, acknowledge their experience, and offer to make it right. That response isn't just for them; it's for every future customer who reads it.

Mistake #4: The Set-It-and-Forget-It Mentality

What it looks like

You set up your profile when you started your business, and you haven't logged into it since. Your photos are years old (or you have zero). You've never published a Google Post. Your hours might not even be current. The services listed are incomplete or outdated.

Why it hurts

Google pays attention to activity. A profile that hasn't been updated in months tells Google's algorithm that this business might not be very active — or worse, might not even be open anymore. Meanwhile, your competitor who posts weekly updates and adds fresh project photos is signaling to Google that they're alive, active, and relevant. Guess who gets prioritized in search results?

How to fix it

Set a recurring reminder to update your profile. Here's a simple weekly routine that takes about 15 minutes:

  • Upload 2-3 new photos — finished projects, your team at work, your truck, your equipment. Real photos from real jobs.
  • Publish a Google Post — share a recent project, a seasonal tip, a special offer, or a quick update about your business.
  • Check your hours and info — especially around holidays or seasonal changes.
  • Respond to any new reviews — don't let them sit for more than a day or two.

Consistency matters more than perfection here. A simple weekly update beats a quarterly overhaul every time.

Mistake #5: Inconsistent NAP Across the Web

What it looks like

Your Google Business Profile says your address is "123 Main St, Suite 4." Your website says "123 Main Street, Ste. 4." Yelp has your old phone number from three years ago. The BBB listing has a slightly different business name. Your Facebook page shows last year's hours.

Why it hurts

NAP stands for Name, Address, Phone number — the three core pieces of your business identity online. Google cross-references your information across dozens of directories, websites, and databases to verify that your business is legitimate and trustworthy. When your NAP is inconsistent, it creates confusion. Google isn't sure which version is correct, and that uncertainty directly impacts your ability to rank in local search. Every conflicting listing chips away at your credibility in Google's eyes.

How to fix it

Pick one exact version of your business name, address, and phone number. Write it down. Then go through every place your business is listed online and make them all match — exactly. That means the same abbreviations (or lack of them), the same suite number format, the same phone number everywhere. The big ones to check: your website, Google Business Profile, Facebook, Yelp, BBB, Angi, Thumbtack, and any industry-specific directories. This is tedious work, but it makes a real difference.

Key Takeaway

None of these mistakes require a marketing degree to fix. They require attention, consistency, and someone who actually takes the time to do it right. Your Google Business Profile is working for you 24 hours a day — but only if you set it up to succeed. Fix these five issues and you'll be ahead of the majority of your local competitors.

The Bottom Line

These five mistakes are incredibly common, and that's actually good news for you. It means most of your competition is making them too. Fix yours, and you immediately stand out in your local market. Your profile becomes more complete, more trustworthy, and more active than the businesses around you — and Google notices.

You don't need to be a marketing expert. You just need to treat your Google Business Profile like what it actually is: the front door of your business for anyone searching online.
