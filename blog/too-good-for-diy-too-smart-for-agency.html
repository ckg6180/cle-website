Too Good for DIY, Too Smart for a Big Agency

Chris Gallagher March 22, 2026 7 min read

You know that stuck feeling. You're too good at what you do to waste your nights watching YouTube videos about SEO. But you're not big enough — or willing — to drop $3K a month on some agency that treats you like their smallest account.

You're somewhere in the middle. And honestly? That's where most local service businesses live. You're past the "I'll just figure it out" phase, but you're not ready to write a big check to someone who doesn't understand what it's actually like to run your kind of business.

So you do nothing. Or you try a little of everything and get nowhere. Sound familiar?

Let's talk about why every option you've been given so far has felt wrong — and what actually makes sense for a business like yours.

The DIY Trap

You've been there. Google Sheets open in one tab, a YouTube tutorial in another, some random blog post you found at 11pm in a third. You set up your Google Business Profile at some point, maybe asked a few customers for reviews, posted a photo or two... and then life happened.

Not because you're lazy. Because this isn't your job. You have estimates to write, crews to manage, customers to take care of. You've got a business to run.

DIY works for a while. You can get the basics set up, maybe even see a small bump in calls. But for most service businesses, that ceiling hits fast. Your profile goes stale. Your competitors who are actually investing in this stuff start pulling ahead. And you're stuck wondering why your phone stopped ringing as much as it used to.

The hard truth is that Google rewards consistency. It rewards businesses that show up week after week with fresh content, new reviews, updated photos, and optimized listings. That's a part-time job — and you already have a full-time one.

The Big Agency Problem

So you think, okay, maybe it's time to hire someone. You start looking at marketing agencies. They promise the world. Beautiful pitch deck. Case studies that look impressive. A salesperson who really seems to get it.

Then you sign a 6-month contract and reality hits.

Your "dedicated account manager" is a 22-year-old who has never cleaned a gutter, fixed a pipe, or knocked on a door. They're managing 40 other accounts alongside yours. Your questions take 3 days to get answered — if they get answered at all. You're on the hook for $2K-5K a month for reports full of jargon you don't understand and results you can't actually see in your bank account.

Here's what most business owners figure out too late: you're their smallest client. The agency's real attention goes to the big accounts. You get the leftover bandwidth, the junior staff, the templated strategy that gets copy-pasted across every client.

You didn't sign up for that. You signed up because you wanted someone who actually cared about growing your business. Instead, you got a line item on someone else's spreadsheet.

The Bottom-Tier Service Trap

Then there's the other end of the spectrum — the $99/month "GBP optimization" packages you see advertised everywhere. Sounds like a deal, right?

Until you realize what you're actually getting. It's automated junk. Cookie-cutter posts that could be for any business in any city. Stock photos. Generic descriptions. No strategy. No one actually looking at your business, your market, or your competitors.

You're not paying for marketing. You're paying for the illusion of marketing. It looks like something is happening, but nothing is actually moving the needle. Your Google Maps ranking stays flat, your phone doesn't ring any more than it did before, and after a few months you cancel and feel like you wasted your money.

Because you did.

So Where Does That Leave You?

This is the gap that nobody talks about. There's a huge space between "do it yourself" and "hire a big agency" — and it's where most local service businesses actually need to be.

You don't need a massive marketing operation. You don't need a 15-person team with a project manager, a content strategist, and a social media coordinator. You need one person who knows what they're doing, understands your business, and actually does the work.
